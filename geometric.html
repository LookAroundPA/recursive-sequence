What this calculator does

This tool works with finite geometric sequences. It can list the first n terms, add those terms, or calculate a single term directly. A geometric model is appropriate when each term is found by multiplying the previous term by the same value.

Geometric sequence formulas

The nth term is a(n) = a1 * r^(n - 1), where a1 is the first term, r is the common ratio, and n is the position.

When r != 1, the finite sum is S(n) = a1[1 - r^n] / [1 - r]. When r = 1, every term equals the first term, so S(n) = n * a1.

The recursive form is a(n) = r * a(n - 1) for n > 1, together with a stated first term.

Step-by-step instructions

  1. Identify the first term of the sequence.
  2. Find the common ratio by dividing a term by the nonzero term immediately before it.
  3. To list terms and find their sum, enter a positive whole number in “Number of terms.”
  4. To find one term, enter its positive whole-number position in the nth-term section.
  5. Select the appropriate calculation button and check whether the output follows the expected multiplication pattern.

Practical examples

Repeated growth

A quantity starts at 100 and grows by 10% each period. Use a1 = 100 and r = 1.1. The fourth value is 100(1.1)^3 = 133.1.

Folding thickness

A sheet is 0.1 mm thick and doubles with every fold. With r = 2, the sixth listed thickness is 0.1(2)^5 = 3.2 mm.

Alternating values

The sequence 8, -4, 2, -1 has r = -0.5. A negative ratio alternates the sign while its absolute value controls growth or decay.

Common mistakes

  • Subtracting consecutive terms instead of dividing to find the ratio.
  • Using the percentage change as the ratio; 10% growth has a ratio of 1.10, not 0.10.
  • Using the exponent n instead of n - 1 in the nth-term formula.
  • Ignoring a negative ratio, which causes term signs to alternate.
  • Applying the infinite-sum formula to a finite-sum question.

Geometric sequence FAQ

Can a common ratio be zero?

Yes. After the first term, every later term will be zero. Be careful when trying to infer a ratio from terms that already equal zero, because division by zero is undefined.

What does a ratio between 0 and 1 do?

For a positive first term, a positive ratio between 0 and 1 produces terms that decrease toward zero.

When does an infinite geometric series have a sum?

It converges only when the absolute value of the common ratio is less than 1. This calculator currently reports finite sums only.

How do I find the common ratio?

Divide a term by the nonzero term immediately before it, and check another pair to confirm that the ratio is constant.

Why do large inputs produce very large results?

Exponential growth can become large quickly. Extremely large powers may exceed the numeric range available in the browser.
