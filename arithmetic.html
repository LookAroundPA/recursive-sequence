What this calculator does

This tool works with finite arithmetic sequences. It can list the first n terms, add those terms, or calculate a single term without generating every earlier value. An arithmetic sequence is appropriate when the difference between each pair of consecutive terms stays constant.

Arithmetic sequence formulas

The nth term is a(n) = a1 + (n - 1)d, where a1 is the first term, d is the common difference, and n is the position.

The sum of the first n terms is S(n) = n(a1 + a(n)) / 2. An equivalent formula is S(n) = n[2a1 + (n - 1)d] / 2.

The recursive form is a(n) = a(n - 1) + d for n > 1, together with a stated first term.

Step-by-step instructions

  1. Identify the first term. This is the starting number, not necessarily the smallest number.
  2. Find the common difference by subtracting one term from the next.
  3. To list terms and find their sum, enter a positive whole number in “Number of terms.”
  4. To find one term, enter its positive whole-number position in the nth-term section.
  5. Select the appropriate calculation button and review the result.

Practical examples

Weekly savings

You save $10 in week 1 and increase the weekly amount by $5. With a1 = 10 and d = 5, week 8 is 10 + 7(5) = 45.

Rows of seats

A small venue has 18 seats in the first row and 2 more in every later row. The first 6 rows are 18, 20, 22, 24, 26, and 28, for a total of 138 seats.

Decreasing temperature

A controlled process starts at 80 degrees and drops by 4 degrees per stage. Here d = -4, so the fifth value is 80 + 4(-4) = 64.

Common mistakes

  • Using the second term as the first term when assigning a1.
  • Reversing the subtraction used to find the common difference.
  • Using n instead of n - 1 in the nth-term formula.
  • Entering a decimal or zero for the number of terms or term position.
  • Confusing a sequence of terms with the sum of those terms.

Arithmetic sequence FAQ

Can an arithmetic sequence decrease?

Yes. A negative common difference produces a decreasing arithmetic sequence.

Can the common difference be zero?

Yes. When d = 0, every term is equal to the first term.

How do I find the common difference?

Subtract an earlier term from the term immediately after it. Repeat with another pair to confirm that the difference is constant.

Does the calculator find an infinite sum?

No. It calculates finite sums. A nonzero constant arithmetic sequence does not have a convergent infinite sum.

Why might the result show a decimal?

The calculator accepts decimal starting values and differences. It rounds long floating-point results to four decimal places for readability.
